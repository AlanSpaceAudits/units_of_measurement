\section*{Preface: Internal Consistency}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
\itshape ``An ideal system of fundamental constants would be one in which
these theoretical relationships were satisfied rigorously, while the adopted
value of each individual constant agreed with its observed value.''
\par\vspace{0.3em}
\normalfont\footnotesize\color{dimtext}
W. de Sitter \& D. Brouwer (1938), \emph{On the System of Astronomical
Constants}, p.~213. De Sitter adds, p.~213: \textbf{``our present system is
not consistent.''}
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
A distance is never measured directly. It is computed from an angle and a
known length, by trigonometry: a triangle is fixed only when \textbf{two of
its three} parts are known. Feed in a radius $R$ and an angle, and a distance
comes out. Change the unit of $R$ and every distance changes by the same
factor. The unit is a free choice; the geometry is not. This brief runs the
whole derivation in two units, the Chinese \textbf{li} and the metric
\textbf{metre}, and shows the two results convert exactly into each other.

% ===================== PART I =====================
\partdiv{Part I: The Chinese System}

\section{The angle: the du}

The Chinese divided one full turn of the sky into \textbf{365.25 du}, one du
for each day the Sun drifts through the stars in a year. One du is therefore
just under one degree. Each du holds 10 fen.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$1\;\text{du} = \dfrac{360^\circ}{365.25} = 0.9856^\circ = 59.14'$
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The length: the li}

The li is the Chinese unit of ground distance ($1\;\text{li} = 300\;\text{bu}$).
In 724~AD the astronomer Yi Xing tied it to the sky: marching north along a
ground meridian, the pole star climbs \textbf{1 du for every 351.267 li} (Beer et al., 1961).
Closed into a full circle of $365.25$~du, the survey gives the circumference
of the heavens in li, and the radius follows from $C = 2\pi R$.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$\text{celestial circle} = 351.267 \times 365.25 \approx 128{,}300\;\text{li}
\qquad
R = \dfrac{\text{circle}}{2\pi} \approx 20{,}420\;\text{li}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
\textbf{Gai Tian cosmology.} This is the Chinese canopy-heaven model,
\emph{Gai Tian} (Cullen, 1996; Needham, 1959): the ground is a flat plane and
the li measures travel across
it; the du divides the sky, the Heavens turning around the observer. The circle
and radius $R$ above are dimensions of the celestial sphere, not of a
terrestrial globe. The arc is in the sky, a matter of optics; the ground
itself does not curve. The frame is observer-centric, each observer at the
centre of his own sky.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The Moon and the Sun, in li}

Parallax turns an angle into a distance. A body at distance $d$, seen against
the radius $R$ from Section~2, subtends an angle $\theta$ fixed by the
relation below; supply $R$ and that angle and the distance follows. Both
angles used here are parallax angles. $\theta_{\text{moon}}$ is the Moon's
horizontal parallax, the shift in its apparent place seen across a baseline
$R$. $\theta_{\text{sun}}$ is the solar parallax, fixed from timing a transit
of Venus or Mercury across the Sun's face.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$d = \dfrac{R}{\sin\theta}$
\\[12pt]
$\text{Moon}\;(\theta_{\text{moon}} = 57'):\quad
 d = \dfrac{20{,}420}{\sin 57'} \approx 1{,}231{,}600\;\text{li}$
\\[14pt]
$\text{Sun}\;(\theta_{\text{sun}} = 8.79''):\quad
 d = \dfrac{20{,}420}{\sin 8.79''} \approx 4.789\times10^{8}\;\text{li}
 \;=\; 1\;\text{AU}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
Kepler's third law gives each planet's orbit as a fixed ratio to Earth's.
Its distance is that ratio times the AU.

\section{The speed of light and aberration, in li}

The speed of light is a property of the vacuum, $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\,\varepsilon_0}$.
Measured against li-based units it comes to about $959{,}787$~li per second.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$c = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\,\varepsilon_0}} \approx 959{,}787\;\text{li/s}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
\textbf{Aberration route.} Earth's assumed motion is said to tilt starlight
by the aberration angle $\kappa$ (Bradley, 1728), with
$\tan\kappa = v_\oplus/c$ and $v_\oplus = 2\pi\,\text{AU}/T$, where $T$ is the
year. Solved for the AU, this returns the Earth--Sun distance from $c$ and
$\kappa$ alone, the route Foucault ran in 1862 from a measured $c$ and
Bradley's $\kappa$ (Foucault, 1862).

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$v_\oplus = c\,\tan\kappa \approx 95.37\;\text{li/s}
\qquad
1\;\text{AU} = \dfrac{v_\oplus\,T}{2\pi} \approx 4.790\times10^{8}\;\text{li}$
\\[8pt]
$\kappa + N + p = 20.50'' + 9.20'' + 50.29'' \approx 79.99''$
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
\textbf{The aberration angle is fixed, not measured.} A telescope records one
quantity: the total stellar oscillation, the trepidation angle
$\kappa+N+p \approx 80''$. Nothing in the observation splits it into
aberration, nutation and precession. The route needs $\kappa$ to be exactly
the slice for which $c\,\tan\kappa$ equals the Kepler velocity
$2\pi\,\text{AU}/T$; that slice is not read off the oscillation, it is
assigned the value that closes the identity. In 1805 Laplace had Delambre
redetermine the light constant from the eclipses of Jupiter's moon Io, and
Delambre returned exactly Bradley's aberration figure (Laplace, 1805). The
aberration angle $\kappa$ and the solar parallax $\pi_\odot$ are both pinned
to the AU, so the aberration route and the parallax route of Section~3
return the same distance: that agreement is built in, not independent
confirmation. In the observer-centric Gai Tian frame the Earth does not move.
The telescope records one angle, the whole oscillation $\kappa+N+p$; the
velocity read from it, $c\,\tan(\kappa+N+p)$, is the speed of nothing.
\end{tcolorbox}

% ===================== PART II =====================
\partdiv{Part II: The Metric System}

\section{The angle and the metre}

The metric angle is the \textbf{degree}: one full turn is 360 degrees.

The \textbf{metre} began in 1791 as $1/10{,}000{,}000$ of the Earth's
meridian quadrant, pole to equator, a length pegged to the pole star as the
li is. The Earth is an oblate spheroid; taking its equatorial circumference,
$40{,}075$~km, the equatorial radius follows.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$\text{equatorial circumference} = 40{,}075\;\text{km}
\qquad
R = \dfrac{40{,}075\;\text{km}}{2\pi} \approx 6{,}378\;\text{km}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
\textbf{On the radius.} The Earth is an oblate spheroid: its equatorial
radius $a = 6{,}378.137$~km exceeds its polar radius $b = 6{,}356.752$~km.
This brief uses the equatorial radius. The commonly quoted $6{,}371$~km is
the IUGG mean radius $R = (2a+b)/3$, an average over the spheroid; it is not
used here.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
\textbf{The two-sphere model.} The globe account drops the observer-centric
sky for a geocentric one. It places a fictitious observer at the centre of
the Earth; this linearises the separate observer hemispheres into one sphere
and sets a terrestrial sphere and the celestial sphere in 1:1 correspondence.
The radius $R$ here is that terrestrial sphere. Star positions and ground
positions are both referred to the single fictitious centre.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The Moon and the Sun, in metres}

The same parallax law $d = R/\sin\theta$, the same observed angles, the
metric radius from Section~5.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$\text{Moon}\;(\theta = 57'):\quad
 d = \dfrac{6{,}378\;\text{km}}{\sin 57'} \approx 384{,}690\;\text{km}$
\\[14pt]
$\text{Sun}\;(\theta = 8.79''):\quad
 d = \dfrac{6{,}378\;\text{km}}{\sin 8.79''}
 \approx 1.496\times10^{8}\;\text{km} \;=\; 1\;\text{AU}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The speed of light and aberration, in metres}

The same vacuum relation, in metric units. Because $\mu_0$ and
$\varepsilon_0$ are measured \textbf{per metre}, $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\,\varepsilon_0}$
comes out in metres per second.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$c = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\,\varepsilon_0}} = 299{,}792{,}458\;\text{m/s}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
The aberration route runs as in Section~4, with $c$ in m/s and $T$ the year:

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$v_\oplus = c\,\tan\kappa \approx 29{,}789\;\text{m/s}
\qquad
1\;\text{AU} = \dfrac{v_\oplus\,T}{2\pi} \approx 1.496\times10^{8}\;\text{km}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Light-time of flight, in metres}

A distance can be restated as the time light takes to cross it. One
light-second is $c \times 1\;\text{s}$; one light-minute is sixty of those.
Dividing any distance by $c$ gives its light-time.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$1\;\text{light-second} \approx 299{,}792\;\text{km}
\qquad
1\;\text{light-minute} \approx 1.799\times10^{7}\;\text{km}$
\\[8pt]
$1\;\text{AU} \approx 8.32\;\text{light-minutes}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The metre and \texorpdfstring{$c$}{c}: an algebra switch}

Until 1983 the metre was the standard and $c$ was measured against it. In
1983 $c$ was fixed at exactly $299{,}792{,}458$~m/s and the metre was
redefined as the distance light travels in $1/c$ seconds.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$\dfrac{1}{c} = 3.3356\;\text{ns}
\qquad
\text{metre} = c \times \dfrac{1}{c} = 1\;\text{m}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
Light crosses one metre in $3.3356$~ns; multiply that time back by $c$ and
one metre returns, unchanged. Only the quantity held fixed changed. The
length did not.

% ===================== PART III =====================
\partdiv{Part III: Conversion and Equivalence}

\section{Converting li to metres, and the summary}

The li circle measures the heavens; the metric circle measures the
terrestrial sphere. The two-sphere model sets the two in 1:1 correspondence,
so the celestial circumference $128{,}300$~li and Earth's circumference
$40{,}075$~km describe one circle. Dividing gives the conversion factor.

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
$1\;\text{li} = \dfrac{40{,}075{,}000\;\text{m}}{128{,}300\;\text{li}}
 \approx 312.35\;\text{m} = 0.31235\;\text{km}$
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
Every distance is $R$ times a factor built only from angles. The angle
factors are identical in both systems, so multiplying any li value by
$312.35$~m/li reproduces the metre value exactly.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{l r r}
\textbf{Quantity} & \textbf{Chinese (li)} & \textbf{Metric (m / km)}\\
\hline
Radius $R$              & $20{,}420$~li            & $6{,}378$~km\\
Moon distance           & $1.232\times10^{6}$~li   & $384{,}690$~km\\
Sun distance (1 AU)     & $4.789\times10^{8}$~li   & $1.496\times10^{8}$~km\\
AU via aberration       & $4.790\times10^{8}$~li   & $1.496\times10^{8}$~km\\
1 light-minute          & $5.759\times10^{7}$~li   & $1.799\times10^{7}$~km\\
Speed of light $c$      & $959{,}787$~li/s         & $299{,}792{,}458$~m/s\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{tcolorbox}[mathbox]
\textbf{Check.} $\;1{,}231{,}591\;\text{li} \times 312.35\;\text{m/li}
 = 384{,}691{,}000\;\text{m} = 384{,}691\;\text{km}$ \quad (the Moon, both ways)
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent
The li ladder and the metric ladder are one ladder, read through the
$312.35$ factor. Neither unit is more correct than the other.

\section*{Closing}

Each distance here is fixed by one chosen radius and a set of measured
angles. Run the calculation in li or in metres and only the labels change;
convert between them and every value matches. A system of mutually reliant
ratios is internally consistent by construction. As de Sitter noted, that is
not the same as being independently verified.
